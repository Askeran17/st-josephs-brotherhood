"Lord Jesus Christ, have mercy on me!"

“Keep that prayer going! Lord Jesus Christ, have mercy on me! Lord Jesus Christ, have mercy on me! This prayer will save you. The name of Christ will enlighten you, help you, fill everything that you lack, grow you, become everything to you. If you do not say this prayer, it means that you have not made a good start.”


Saint Joseph the Hesychast

Biography by Joseph the Hesychast


Joseph the Hesychast (Silent Man, worldly name - Frangiskos Kottis) was born on the island of Paros, in the village of Lefke, on November 2, 1897. Frangiskos's parents, Georgios and Maria, were simple workers, but lived a righteous life. They taught all their children to be pious. After Georgios' death in 1907, his widowed wife, Maria, took charge of their upbringing. There were six children and, due to the loss of their breadwinner, the family had to face numerous difficulties.


When Frangiskos was still a small child, God announced to Mary about his future glory through Revelation: the Heavenly messenger who appeared to her then inscribed the name of her son in some mysterious list, after which he took him with him, explaining that it was so pleasing to the Heavenly King. Due to the death of his father and the need to help the family, Frangiskos did not have time to complete the initial course of study at school. In 1914 he went to Piraeus in search of work. Then he was called up for military service. After demobilization, he got a job in Athens. According to some sources, he worked there as a cook, and after some time as a tram conductor. Other evidence suggests that in Athens he was engaged in commercial affairs.


Athos

When Frangiskos reached the age of twenty-three, he showed a keen interest in spiritual literature. Inspired by the lives of the saints of God, he began to imitate them to the best of his ability. During this period, in 1921, he met an elder who gave him a number of good advice. Thanks to this acquaintance, Frangiskos's heart was inclined to choose the monastic path. Soon, imitating the example of the holy fathers and the disposition of his own heart, he distributed his estate to the poor and left for Athos.


Life on Athos

Arriving at the Holy Mountain, he expected to meet here many glorious ascetics, whom he had read about in the Lives, but instead he was disappointed. It was partly due to obviously exaggerated expectations, but partly to the decline in the general level of the moral state of the Athonite monks, which was far removed from the level of piety of pilgrims of previous centuries. According to the memoirs of Joseph the Hesychast himself, this situation brought him into a state of mournful crying.


At first, Frangiskos joined the supporters of Elder Daniel of Katunaki, and for some time labored in their brotherhood, but some time later he wished to find a more secluded residence for himself and left the brotherhood. For a long time he was unable to submit himself to obedience to an experienced confessor. After many unsuccessful attempts, relying on God's Providence, Frangiskos indulged in hermitage. Local caves served as his refuge. He earned his living by working: in particular, by making brooms. During his journey through the lands of Athos, Frangiskos met and then became friends with a like-minded person, the monk Arsenios. Soon, taking advantage of the recommendation of Daniel of Katunaki, who reminded them of the role of obedience and cutting off self-will in monastic work, they came under the teaching and obedience of the Albanian elder, Ephraim of Katunaki.


Monastic feat

In 1925, Frangiskos, having overcome the trials of temptation and labor, was awarded tonsure into the great schema with a new name: Joseph. After Elder Ephraim passed away, Joseph assumed all responsibilities for leading and managing the activities of the community. Left without an elder mentor, the brothers in Christ, Arseny and Joseph, continued to wander around the territory of the Holy Mountain. They returned to Kaliva only in winter, but subsequently chose it as their place of permanent residence.


As follows from the confession of Joseph the Hesychast, at this stage of his asceticism he experienced very strong temptations from fallen spirits. One day he contemplated in a vision a formation of monks: soldiers of Christ, preparing to enter into battle with demonic hordes. The military leader leading the monks invited Joseph to take a place among the advanced fighters, which he did. After this incident, the devil launched a fierce, irreconcilable battle against Joseph: he plotted, lying in wait in ambush; the enemy tried to lure him into shackles and nets with shameless deception. But with the help of God, Joseph successfully overcame enemy attacks. This period of struggle lasted about 8 years.


One of the most significant events at this stage of his monastic life was the acquisition of a new mentor, the humble and wise silent man, Daniel, who labored near the Great Lavra, in the cell of St. Peter of Athos. Joseph adopted many positive traits from him. Imitating his ascetic exploits, he accustomed himself to even greater austerity of life, for example, limiting food consumption to one meal a day (Joseph’s diet then consisted of a measure of bread and a few vegetables). With even greater desire, he began to fight his own laziness.


While Joseph the Hesychast asceticised in the monastery of St. Basil the Great, many ascetics rallied around his personality and a monastic brotherhood was formed. Among the other participants in this brotherhood was his blood brother, Athanasius. Gradually the name of Joseph became more and more famous. Many turned to him for advice and admonitions. Joseph willingly shared his wealth of knowledge, but the crowds associated with incessant visits violated the solitary nature of his personal life and the lives of his brothers. As a result, he was forced to think about looking for a new place. In 1929-30, Elder Joseph left Athos for some time to tonsure his own mother as a monk and found a nunnery in the Drama region. Returning to the Holy Mountain, he did not break off contact with the nuns he had tonsured and continued to instruct them through regular correspondence.


In 1938, Joseph the Hesychast, together with the monk Arseny, drew attention to one abandoned kaliva and chose it as the next place for ascetic deeds. Kalyva was located in the Small Skete of St. Anna, in caves under a mountain cliff. From available materials, clay and wood, the brethren built themselves a modest hut, which included three small rooms, cells. One of them was intended for Joseph, the other for his comrade-in-arms, Arseny. The third was used by the hieromonk who visited them. In addition, the brethren restored the Church of St. John the Baptist on that site. They labored in kaliva for 30 years. Initially, they prayed and worked together. Both the lack of space for living quarters and the not entirely convenient location of the kaliva had an impact. Subsequently, other ascetics began to join them, mainly young monks. The brotherhood grew.


Over time, Elder Joseph decided to move closer to the coastline. The Kaliva of the Holy Unmercenaries, located in the New Skete, was chosen as an alternative residence. In 1958, Father Joseph suffered from a serious illness. First, a dangerous abscess formed on his neck. He then suffered from heart failure. At first, he did not agree to undergo a course of treatment - he did not want to tear himself away from his monastic deeds - but then, yielding to the persuasion of his spiritual students, he agreed. He felt the approach of death in advance. Shortly before his death, on the Day of the Dormition of the Mother of God, he received communion of the Holy Mysteries of Christ.


On August 15, 1959, the ascetic’s heart stopped. On March 9, 2020, the Patriarchate of Constantinople canonized him as a saint with the establishment of his memory on August 16.

Biography by Josephs disciples


Elder Arsenios

Father Arsenios Hesychast (Galanopoulos; 1886–1983) is an Athonite elder who acquired the gifts of spiritual reasoning, humility and meekness, insight and unceasing prayer. For almost forty years, Father Arseny was an inseparable companion, spiritual brother and companion of the great elder Reverend Joseph the Hesychast (1897–1959). Elder Arsenios reposed in the Lord on September 15, 1983.


Elder Ephraim of Katunaki

Elder Hieroschemamonk Ephraim Katunaksky (Papanikitas; 1912–1998) - disciple of Elder Joseph the Hesychast, ascetic of Holy Mount Athos. During his lifetime, he became widely known as the continuer of the ancient ascetic tradition of hesychast prayer. Father Ephraim lived on the Holy Mountain for sixty-five years (of which forty-two years were as a novice) and glorified holy obedience throughout his life. Elder Ephraim reposed in the Lord on February 27, 1998.


Elder Joseph of Vatopedi

Elder Schemamonk Joseph was born in 1921 on the island of Cyprus into a large peasant family. He was a disciple of Elder Joseph the Hesychast. Elder Joseph reposed in the Lord at dawn on July 1, 2009. An hour and a half after the death of Elder Joseph, a miracle happened: when the monks revealed the elder’s face for the last time, everyone witnessed the blissful smile that appeared on the lips of the ascetic. The deceased smiled, thereby “effectively capturing the evidence of the general resurrection,” according to the rector of Vatopedi, Archimandrite Ephraim.


Elder Charalampius

Elder Charalampius was born in 1910 into a family of pious parents. From a young age, he was repeatedly tested by God for the strength of his faith and was awarded the salvation of his life by the miraculous appearance of St. George the Victorious during Bulgarian captivity. Together with his parents, Charalampius experienced to the full the bitterness of the Turkish genocide, the misfortunes of war, the hardships of forced emigration and many other sorrows. Elder Charalampius became a disciple of Elder Joseph in 1950. Elder Charalampius reposed in the Lord on January 1, 2001.


Elder Ephraim of Arizona

Father Ephraim is a spiritual child, novice and companion of Elder Joseph the Hesychast, who was able to fully perceive the rich spiritual experience of his great teacher. The Lord called Father Ephraim to the path of monasticism in his youth and from the very beginning in a special way testified to His care for this newly chosen warrior of Christ. “I was nineteen years old,” the elder writes in his memoirs, “when I set out on the path to the inheritance of the Mother of God, to the Holy Mountain. This path to monastic life was shown to me by my virtuous and monastic-loving mother, now nun Feofania.” Next to his mentor, Father Ephraim spent twelve years - as long as Father Joseph had lived since their first meeting - literally absorbing his teachings and instructions. The Lord vouchsafed the novice to serve his geronda until his very last breath. By special instructions from God, Father Ephraim moved to the USA, where in the state of Arizona, in the desert, he founded a monastery in honor of St. Anthony the Great. In total, there are twenty monasteries under his care in America and Canada today. Elder Ephraim reposed in the Lord on December 7, 2019.
